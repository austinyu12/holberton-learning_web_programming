The Problem

One huge problem humanity is dealing with is the scarcity of water which will only increase as time goes on. Recently, we have seen more drastic weather events: longer droughts, stronger typhoons, and more frequent earthquakes for example. These events are leading to less predictable weather forecasts. In other words, access to potable water will become less and less consistent and existing access to potable water will become a less reliable source for water. "The 2019 UN World Water Development report notes that about 4 billion people, representing nearly two-thirds of the world population, experience severe water scarcity during at least one month of the year. With rising demand, the quality and supply of water diminishes." according to Wikipedia, a credible source. Furthermore, when the water we do have is used, it returns to the environment in worse condition than before (This goes against the American thought of Leave No Trace.) According to UNESCO, "Worldwide, over 80% of all wastewater returns to the environment without being treated." Contaminated water returning to the environment is bad for our ecosystems, but how is it bad for humans? It effects our food supply and vacation hotspots: diseased fish and dirty beaches. On the other hand, there are many that still do not have access to water, so shouldn't we consume water more conservatively?

Why Hasn't Been Solved Yet

As mentioned before, water scarcity in urban places such as California, is caused by the growing inconsistency of rain, by the heavy reliance on resevoirs that rely on rain water, and by the unconservative usage of potable water. In rural countries, water scarcity is a problem usually due to a lack of infrastructure, constant conflict preventing the leaders of countries to focusing on this issue and discouraging investing in building infrastructure, and due to the lack of equipment, knowledge, and ability to safely build structures such as dams, resevoirs, or water treatment plants.

How The Problem Is Mitigated

According to UNESCO, dry areas will become dryer and wet areas wetter, and we decided to use this to our advantage. In tropical regions of the world, monsoons, typhoons, and flooding are occuring more often with stronger force. For example, existing infrastructure to deal with flooding may not be adequate. In order to deal with that, we have decided to set up facilities in these regions to collect the water, treat the water, and ship the water to places in drought or lack potable water. There is one problem, water is very dense and transporting it without pipelines is very expensive. However, building pipelines take tremendous amounts of time and resources and this is a problem that needs to be addressed immediately. When water distribution checkpoints are made to avoid building infrastruction, the recipients of the water may have to walk long distances to get to the checkpoint, wait in line, and carry the dense water back home. We have found a way to solve the problem: dehydrated water. By dehydrating water, it is less dense, allowing planes to make faster trips (due to less weight) and consume less fuel. The benefit of faster trips is that it can make more trips, increasing the possible amount of distribution points and the amount of water shipped. The benefit of less fuel consumed is that it is cheaper to make these trips and there is less financial loss when care package aircraft need to fly over conflict zones.

How To Use The Product

First, pour the Hydrate or Diedrate™ Dehydrated Water into a container twice the size of the can or retort pouch. In order to activate the Hydrate or Diedrate™ Dehydrated Water, a 1:1 ratio of water is needed. After adding water to the Hydrate or Diedrate™ Dehydrated Water, mix until all the Hydrate or Diedrate™ Dehydrated Water is hydrated. Lastly, consume (or inhale if you prefer to do that).
